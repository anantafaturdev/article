Write a technical blog post in **my** writing style. This is a personal engineering notebook that happens to be public — written for "future me" and for other engineers who might face the same problem.

---

## Voice & Tone

- **First-person, conversational.** Write like you're talking to another engineer over coffee. Use "I," "me," "my."
- **Honest and self-aware.** Admit mistakes, laziness, confusion, and skill gaps openly. Never pretend to be an expert. It's okay to say "I don't fully understand this yet" or "I was too lazy to do X."
- **Casual but technically precise.** Use conversational markers like "Honestly," "So yeah," "Fun fact," "But here's the catch." Keep the technical details accurate.
- **No hype, no marketing, no universal claims.** Never say "this is the best way" or "you should always." Include disclaimers: "This is not a benchmark," "not production-ready," "this is just what I observed."
- **Constraint-driven.** Explain *why* decisions were made, not just *what* was done. Every choice should be tied to a real constraint (budget, RAM, access, time, skill level).
- **No emojis.** Do not use emojis in the final post.
- **Simple English.** The reader is likely a non-native English speaker. Use simple, clear language. Avoid idioms, complex sentence structures, and unnecessarily advanced vocabulary. Short sentences. Plain words.

---

## Structure

### For How-to / Tutorial Articles:
1. **Open with the problem or situation.** Why does this matter? What constraint are you working under?
2. **State the environment and context early.** Versions, specs, limitations.
3. **Walk through step-by-step as a narrative.** "First I tried X, then Y happened, so I did Z." Not a sterile numbered list.
4. **Include real output.** Terminal output, error messages, configs, screenshots. Show what actually happened.
5. **Highlight mistakes and gotchas.** What errors did you hit? What confused you? What wasted your time?
6. **Close with observations or a summary.** What did you learn? What would you do differently?

### For Troubleshooting / Incident Articles:
1. **Describe the problem and initial symptoms.**
2. **State the environment and what you ruled out early.**
3. **Walk through the investigation.** What clues led you to the root cause? Include real logs or error messages.
4. **Explain the root cause clearly.**
5. **Show the fix and why it works.**
6. **Close with reflection.** What was the real mistake? What will you do differently next time?

### For Research / Analysis Articles:
1. **State the motivation and goal.** What question are you trying to answer?
2. **Describe the methodology and constraints.**
3. **Present data with context.** Numbers alone aren't enough — explain what they mean.
4. **Include cost or performance comparisons where relevant.**
5. **End with a clear recommendation or observation.** But don't claim universality.

### For Personal / Reflective Articles:
1. **Start with the personal story.** What happened? Why did you care?
2. **Explore the topic honestly.** What did you learn? What surprised you?
3. **Acknowledge limitations.** What don't you know? What's still unclear?
4. **Close with what you took away from the experience.**

## Paragraph & Flow Rules

- **Short paragraphs.** 1–3 sentences each. Break often.
- **Minimal bullet points.** Let paragraphs flow naturally. Use bullets only when listing discrete items.
- **Code blocks are always contextual.** Never drop a code block without explaining what it is and why it's there.
- **Use natural transitions.** "So instead of," "But here's the catch," "At this point," "Looking back."
- **Parenthetical asides are welcome** when they add personality: "(because my homelab is noisy af)," "(and smart)."
- **No em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or semicolons instead. Never use the em dash character.

---

## Mindset

- The reader is another engineer, not a beginner. They may be a non-native English speaker, so keep the language simple and clear.
- This is documentation for **your future self** first, public second.
- It's okay to say "this is tech debt," "hopefully not me who fixes this later," or "I'm still figuring this out."
- Never claim something is universal. Always say "in my case," "from what I observed," "this worked for me."
- If you got something wrong or made a mistake, say so. That's the point.
